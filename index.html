Philosophies of Humankind, before 300 BCE

A branching tree of the world's earliest wisdom — cosmology, ethics, metaphysics and law — across seven civilizations, with each thinker's major writings and links to the original texts online.

“The owl of Minerva spreads its wings only with the falling of the dusk.” — and yet here, at the dawn, humankind first asked: What is real? How should I live? What is just?

This tree gathers the major philosophical traditions that arose before 300 BCE. “Philosophy” is taken broadly — not only the systematic argument of the Greeks, but the wisdom literature, cosmologies, ethical and legal reflection, and metaphysical insight that earlier and contemporary civilizations produced. Each entry gives the thinker or text in English and in its own language/script (with romanization), approximate dates, and a 📜 link to an online edition of the original text wherever one survives.

The Axial Age. The philosopher Karl Jaspers named the centuries c. 800–300 BCE the “Achsenzeit” (Axial Age): in this window, and seemingly independently, China, India, Persia, Israel and Greece each gave birth to enduring philosophies and religions. Older still are the wisdom traditions of Mesopotamia and Egypt, whose reflections on justice, mortality and cosmic order (Ma’at) form the deep root of this tree.

  • 🌍 Philosophies of Humankind — before 300 BCE
    • ▶𒀭 Mesopotamia Sumer · Akkad · Babylon c. 2600 – 500 BCE The first writing, the first laws, the first poems on death and justice — proto-philosophy in cuneiform.
      • Sumerian & Akkadian Wisdom 𒅗𒅗 nam-ku3-zu Instruction, proverb and lament — reflection on fate, conduct and the gods.
        • Instructions of Shuruppak 𒋗𒆳𒆠 šuruppak c. 2600–2500 BCE
          Among the oldest literary texts on Earth: a father’s practical & ethical maxims to his son, anticipating later wisdom books by millennia.
          • Sumerian instruction poem ETCSL (Sumerian + English)
        • The Epic of Gilgamesh 𒄑𒂅𒈠 / Sha naqba īmuru c. 2100–1200 BCE
          Humankind’s first meditation on mortality, friendship and the limits of glory: a king seeks eternal life and learns to accept death.
          • Standard Babylonian Epic Internet Sacred Text Archive ancienttexts.org
        • Babylonian wisdom dialogues Ludlul bēl nēmeqi · Babylonian Theodicy · Dialogue of Pessimism c. 1500–1000 BCE
          The problem of undeserved suffering and the silence of the gods — a Mesopotamian “Job” and a startling dialogue on the futility of every plan.
          • Akkadian wisdom texts Sacred Texts: Ancient Near East translations of Lambert’s Babylonian Wisdom Literature
      • Law & Cosmic Order Justice (Sumerian níg-si-sá) as a divine mandate held by the king.
        • The Code of Hammurabi 𒄩𒄠𒈬𒊏𒁉 Ḫammurabi c. 1754 BCE
          One of the earliest written legal philosophies — ~282 laws “that the strong might not oppress the weak,” framing justice as cosmic order.
          • Law-code stele Yale Avalon Project
        • Enūma Eliš (Creation Epic) 𒂊𒉡𒈠𒂊𒇺 Enūma Eliš c. 1200–1100 BCE
          A cosmogony: order (Marduk) wrought out of primordial chaos (Tiamat) — the world and humankind given purpose and rank.
          • Babylonian creation myth Internet Sacred Text Archive
    • ▶𓂀 Ancient Egypt Kemet · 𓆎𓅓𓏏𓊖 c. 2400 – 1100 BCE The Sebayt (“Teachings”) and the ideal of Ma’at — truth, balance and cosmic justice.
      • Ma’at & the Teachings (Sebayt) 𓐙𓏏𓆄 mꜣꜥt Ethics as alignment with Ma’at: truth, order, reciprocity — weighed against the heart after death.
        • Ptahhotep 𓊪𓏏𓎛𓊪 Ptḥ-ḥtp vizier, c. 2400 BCE
          The Maxims of Ptahhotep — perhaps the world’s oldest book of moral philosophy: 37 instructions on humility, justice, speech and self-control.
          • The Instruction of Ptah-hotep Project Gutenberg (Gunn tr.) Sacred Texts
        • Instruction of Amenemope jmn-m-jpt c. 1300–1100 BCE
          Thirty chapters on the “silent, contented man,” honesty and contentment — closely parallel to the Biblical Book of Proverbs.
          • Wisdom of Amenemope Sacred Texts: Egypt in collections of Egyptian wisdom
        • Dispute between a Man & his Ba Papyrus Berlin 3024 c. 1900 BCE
          A man debates his own soul about whether life’s suffering makes death desirable — a strikingly existential dialogue.
          • “Dispute of a man with his soul” Sacred Texts: Egypt
        • The Great Hymn to the Aten attr. Akhenaten c. 1350 BCE
          A radical, near-monotheistic hymn to the sun-disc as sole creator — often compared with Psalm 104.
          • Great Hymn to the Aten full translation
        • Book of the Dead 𓂋𓏤𓈒𓂧 rꜣ nw prt m hrw New Kingdom, c. 1550 BCE–
          “Spells of Coming Forth by Day” — including the weighing of the heart against the feather of Ma’at, an early articulation of moral judgment.
          • Papyrus of Ani Sacred Texts (Budge tr.)
    • ▶✡︎ Israel / Hebrew עִבְרִית c. 900 – 300 BCE Ethical monotheism, prophetic justice, and the wisdom of Proverbs, Job & Qohelet.
      • Wisdom Literature חָכְמָה Ḥokhmah The fear of the LORD, the limits of human understanding, and the search for meaning “under the sun.”
        • Proverbs מִשְׁלֵיMishlei compiled c. 7th–5th c. BCE
          Practical and theological wisdom; personified Wisdom (Ḥokhmah) as the first of God’s works.
          • Sefer Mishlei Sefaria (Hebrew + English)
        • Ecclesiastes (Qoheleth) קֹהֶלֶתQohelet c. 450–200 BCE
          “Vanity of vanities” — a profound, skeptical meditation on mortality, time and the meaning of toil.
          • Sefer Qohelet Sefaria (Hebrew + English)
        • Job אִיּוֹבIyyov c. 6th–4th c. BCE
          The drama of the righteous sufferer; the problem of theodicy answered from the whirlwind.
          • Sefer Iyyov Sefaria (Hebrew + English)
      • Torah & the Prophets תּוֹרָה · נְבִיאִים Covenant, law and the prophetic demand for justice and mercy over ritual.
        • The Torah תּוֹרָהTorah redacted c. 6th–5th c. BCE
          The Five Books — creation, covenant and the moral law (the Decalogue) at the root of Western ethics.
          • Chamishah Chumshei Torah Sefaria · Genesis full Tanakh
        • The Prophets (Amos, Isaiah…) נְבִיאִיםNevi’im 8th–6th c. BCE
          Amos and Isaiah forge ethical monotheism: “let justice roll down like waters” — righteousness above sacrifice.
          • Sefer Amos · Yeshayahu Sefaria · Amos Isaiah
    • ▶🔥 Persia / Iran ایران date debated · c. 1200–600 BCE Cosmic dualism, free moral choice, and a final judgment — Zoroaster’s revolution.
      • Zoroastrianism مزدیسنا Mazdayasna Asha (truth/order) against Druj (the lie); “good thoughts, good words, good deeds.”
        • Zoroaster (Zarathustra) زرتشت Avestan: Zaraθuštra c. 1200–600 BCE
          Prophet who reframed religion as a moral struggle between Ahura Mazda (Wise Lord) and Angra Mainyu — with human will as the deciding force, and history moving toward renewal.
          • The Gathas گاهان Gāθā avesta.org (Avestan + English)
          • The Avesta (Yasna) اوستا Sacred Texts · Yasna (SBE 31) full Avesta
    • ▶🕉️ India भारत · Bhārata c. 1500 – 300 BCE Brahman & Ātman, karma & rebirth, liberation (mokṣa/nirvāṇa) — and India’s great skeptics.
      • Vedic & Upaniṣadic Tradition वेद · उपनिषद् From ritual hymns to the inward turn: the identity of Self (Ātman) and Absolute (Brahman).
        • The Vedas वेदVeda c. 1500–900 BCE
          The four Vedas (esp. Ṛgveda) — hymn, sacrifice and the first cosmological speculation (the Nāsadīya “Hymn of Creation”).
          • Ṛgvedaऋग्वेद Sacred Texts (Griffith tr.)
        • The Upaniṣads उपनिषद्Upaniṣad c. 800–300 BCE
          The foundation of Vedānta: Tat tvam asi (“That thou art”), the unity of Ātman and Brahman, karma and rebirth. Principal texts: Bṛhadāraṇyaka, Chāndogya, Īśa, Kaṭha, Kena, Muṇḍaka.
          • Principal Upaniṣads Sacred Texts (SBE 1) SBE 15
      • The Śramaṇa Movement श्रमण Renouncer traditions outside Vedic authority — Jainism, Buddhism, Ājīvika.
        • Mahāvīra (Jainism) महावीर / वर्धमान Vardhamāna c. 599–527 BCE
          24th Tīrthaṅkara of Jainism: radical non-violence (ahiṃsā), many-sidedness of truth (anekāntavāda), and liberation of the soul (jīva) from karmic matter.
          • Jain Sūtras (Āgamas) — Ācārāṅga, Kalpa Sacred Texts (SBE 22 & 45)
        • The Buddha (Buddhism) सिद्धार्थ गौतम / बुद्ध Siddhārtha Gautama c. 563–483 BCE
          The Four Noble Truths and the Eightfold Path; impermanence (anicca), non-self (anattā) and dependent origination — a middle way to the cessation of suffering (nibbāna).
          • Dhammapadaधम्मपद (Pāli) Sacred Texts (SBE 10)
          • Pāli Canon (Tipiṭaka) Sacred Texts: Buddhism
        • Makkhali Gosāla (Ājīvika) मक्खलि गोसाल fl. c. 500 BCE
          Founder of the Ājīvikas: strict determinism (niyati) — all is fixed by fate, effort changes nothing. Known mainly through rival Buddhist & Jain accounts.
          • via Buddhist/Jain sources (Sāmaññaphala Sutta) Sacred Texts: Buddhism
      • Heterodox & Analytic Schools दर्शन darśana Materialism, dualist analysis, and the science of language.
        • Cārvāka / Lokāyata चार्वाक / लोकायत attr. Bṛhaspati; pre-300 BCE
          India’s materialist, empiricist school: only perception is valid knowledge; no soul, no afterlife. Its Bārhaspatya-sūtras are lost — preserved only in opponents’ refutations.
          • Bārhaspatya-sūtras (fragments) survive only in quotation
        • Kapila & Sāṃkhya कपिल · सांख्य tradition pre-300 BCE
          One of the oldest darśanas: a dualism of consciousness (puruṣa) and primal matter (prakṛti), and an enumeration of reality’s 25 principles.
          • later Sāṃkhya-kārikā Sacred Texts: Hinduism earliest systematic text is later
        • Pāṇini पाणिनि c. 5th–4th c. BCE
          The Aṣṭādhyāyī: a formal, generative grammar of Sanskrit of astonishing rigor — a landmark in the philosophy of language and early formal systems.
          • Aṣṭādhyāyīअष्टाध्यायी Vasu translation (Archive)
    • ▶☯️ China 中國 · Zhōngguó c. 600 – 300 BCE The “Hundred Schools of Thought” (諸子百家) of the Spring & Autumn and Warring States eras.
      • Confucianism 儒家 Rújiā Virtue (rén, 仁), ritual propriety (lǐ, 禮) and the cultivation of the noble person.
        • Confucius 孔子Kǒngzǐ 551–479 BCE
          China’s most influential teacher: ethics grounded in humaneness (rén), filial duty and the rectification of names; virtue, not force, as the basis of order.
          • The Analects論語 Lúnyǔ Chinese Text Project (中 + EN)
        • Mencius 孟子Mèngzǐ c. 372–289 BCE
          Argued human nature is innately good; developed the “four sprouts” of virtue and the people’s right against unjust rulers (the Mandate of Heaven).
          • Mencius孟子 Chinese Text Project (中 + EN)
        • Xúnzǐ 荀子Xúnzǐ c. 310–235 BCE ~300
          Countered Mencius: human nature is crooked and must be reshaped by ritual and education. A rigorous, naturalistic Confucian (teacher of the Legalists).
          • Xunzi荀子 Chinese Text Project (中 + EN)
      • Daoism 道家 Dàojiā The Way (dào, 道), spontaneity (zìrán) and effortless action (wúwéi, 無為).
        • Laozi 老子Lǎozǐ trad. 6th c. BCE
          The (semi-legendary) sage of the Dào: the nameless source of all things, the power of yielding and emptiness, and rule by non-interference.
          • Dao De Jing道德經 Dàodéjīng Chinese Text Project (中 + EN)
        • Zhuangzi 莊子Zhuāngzǐ c. 369–286 BCE
          Playful, profound skepticism: the relativity of all perspectives (the butterfly dream), freedom from convention, and at-oneness with transformation.
          • Zhuangzi莊子 Chinese Text Project (中 + EN)
      • Mohism 墨家 Mòjiā Impartial care, consequentialist utility, anti-war — and early formal logic.
        • Mozi 墨子Mòzǐ c. 470–391 BCE
          Preached “impartial love” (jiān’ài) and judged actions by benefit to all; opposed aggressive war and lavish ritual. The Mohist Canon pioneered logic & optics.
          • Mozi墨子 Chinese Text Project (中 + EN)
      • Legalism 法家 Fǎjiā Order through codified law (fǎ), statecraft (shù) and power (shì).
        • Shang Yang 商鞅Shāng Yāng d. 338 BCE
          Reformer of Qin: strict, impartial law and reward/punishment over Confucian virtue — foundations of the bureaucratic state.
          • Book of Lord Shang商君書 Chinese Text Project (中 + EN)
        • Han Feizi 韓非子Hán Fēizǐ c. 280–233 BCE ~300
          The great synthesizer of Legalism: a clear-eyed, often cynical theory of law, bureaucracy and political power that shaped China’s first empire.
          • Han Feizi韓非子 Chinese Text Project (中 + EN)
      • School of Names & the Strategists 名家 · 兵家 Logic and paradox; and the philosophy of conflict.
        • Gongsun Long 公孫龍Gōngsūn Lóng c. 320–250 BCE
          Logician of the School of Names, famous for the paradox “a white horse is not a horse” — probing the relation of name, class and reality.
          • Gongsun Longzi公孫龍子 Chinese Text Project (中 + EN)
        • Sun Tzu 孫子Sūnzǐ c. 544–496 BCE
          The philosophy of strategy: winning without battle, deception, timing and adaptation — a Daoist-tinged classic still studied worldwide.
          • The Art of War孫子兵法 Sūnzǐ Bīngfǎ Chinese Text Project (中 + EN)
      • Foundational Classics 五經 Wǔjīng The shared canon behind every school — cosmology, poetry, history, ritual.
        • Book of Changes (I Ching) 易經Yìjīng core c. 1000–750 BCE
          A divination manual that became a cosmology of change — yin/yang, the eight trigrams, and the patterned flux of all things.
          • I Ching · Book of Songs · Documents Yijing Shijing Shujing
    • ▶🏛️ Greece Ἑλλάς · Hellás c. 600 – 322 BCE From the first “physicists” of Miletus to Aristotle — the birth of systematic reason in the West.
      • Milesian / Ionian School φυσικοί The first to seek a natural archē (origin) of all things, without myth.
        • Thales of Miletus ΘαλῆςThalēs c. 624–546 BCE
          Traditionally the “first philosopher”: proposed water as the fundamental principle and predicted a solar eclipse — explanation by nature, not gods.
          • Testimonia & fragments Hanover Historical Texts
        • Anaximander ἈναξίμανδροςAnaximandros c. 610–546 BCE
          The apeiron (the boundless) as origin; an early evolutionary and cosmological vision. Author of the first Greek prose treatise, On Nature.
          • On Nature (Περὶ φύσεως), fragments Hanover Historical Texts
        • Anaximenes ἈναξιμένηςAnaximenēs c. 586–526 BCE
          Held air (aēr) to be the source, condensing and rarefying into all things — an early mechanism of qualitative change.
          • Fragments Hanover Historical Texts
      • Pythagoreans & Eleatics Πυθαγόρειοι · Ἐλεᾶται Number & soul; and the logic of Being and the One.
        • Pythagoras ΠυθαγόραςPythagóras c. 570–495 BCE
          “All is number”: mathematics as the structure of reality, the harmony of the spheres, and the transmigration of the soul.
          • Testimonia (no writings survive) Hanover Historical Texts
        • Xenophanes ΞενοφάνηςXenophanēs c. 570–478 BCE
          Critic of anthropomorphic gods (“oxen would draw gods like oxen”); pointed toward a single, non-human divine and the limits of knowledge.
          • Fragments Hanover Historical Texts
        • Heraclitus ἩράκλειτοςHērákleitos c. 535–475 BCE
          “Everything flows” (panta rhei): reality is process, unified by the Logos and the tension of opposites. “You cannot step twice into the same river.”
          • On Nature, fragments Wikisource (Greek + English)
        • Parmenides ΠαρμενίδηςParmenidēs c. 515–450 BCE
          Founder of Eleatic metaphysics & logic: Being simply is, one and changeless; change and plurality are illusions of the senses.
          • On Nature (poem), fragments Hanover Historical Texts
        • Zeno of Elea Ζήνων ὁ ἘλεάτηςZēnōn c. 490–430 BCE
          Defended Parmenides with the famous paradoxes of motion (Achilles & the tortoise) — the dawn of rigorous proof and the infinite.
          • Paradoxes, fragments Hanover Historical Texts
      • Pluralists & Atomists φυσιολόγοι Saving the appearances of change after Parmenides — elements, Mind, and atoms.
        • Empedocles ἘμπεδοκλῆςEmpedoklēs c. 494–434 BCE
          Four “roots” (earth, water, air, fire) mixed by Love and parted by Strife — the ancestor of the four-element theory.
          • On Nature · Purifications, fragments Hanover Historical Texts
        • Anaxagoras ἈναξαγόραςAnaxagóras c. 500–428 BCE
          “In everything a portion of everything”; cosmic Nous (Mind) orders the universe — the first to make intelligence a first principle.
          • On Nature, fragments Hanover Historical Texts
        • Leucippus & Democritus Λεύκιππος · ΔημόκριτοςDēmókritos c. 460–370 BCE
          Founders of atomism: nothing exists but atoms and void; a fully materialist, mechanistic cosmos — later inherited by Epicurus.
          • Fragments (atomist) Fairbanks, First Philosophers of Greece
      • Sophists σοφισταί Teachers of rhetoric & virtue; relativism and the human measure.
        • Protagoras ΠρωταγόραςPrōtagóras c. 490–420 BCE
          “Man is the measure of all things” — the founding statement of relativism and humanistic skepticism about the gods.
          • via Plato’s Protagoras Perseus (Greek + English)
        • Gorgias ΓοργίαςGorgías c. 483–375 BCE
          Master rhetorician and radical skeptic: in On Non-Existence, argued nothing exists, or if it does, it can be neither known nor communicated.
          • Encomium of Helen · via Plato’s Gorgias Perseus (Greek + English)
      • The Classical Triad Σωκράτης · Πλάτων · Ἀριστοτέλης Ethics, the theory of Forms, logic and the systematic organization of all knowledge.
        • Socrates ΣωκράτηςSōkrátēs 470–399 BCE
          Turned philosophy to ethics & self-knowledge through relentless questioning (the elenchus): “the unexamined life is not worth living.” Wrote nothing; known via his pupils.
          • Plato’s Apology (Ἀπολογία) MIT Classics Perseus (Greek)
        • Plato ΠλάτωνPlátōn c. 428–348 BCE
          The Theory of Forms, the immortal soul, the tripartite city & psyche, and the philosopher-king. Founder of the Academy; author of the dialogues.
          • Republic (Πολιτεία) Perseus (Greek + English) MIT
          • all dialogues (Phaedo, Symposium, Timaeus…) Perseus · Plato
        • Aristotle ἈριστοτέληςAristotélēs 384–322 BCE
          The most encompassing mind of antiquity: founder of formal logic, biology and systematic ethics. Virtue as the mean; the four causes; the Unmoved Mover.
          • Nicomachean Ethics (Ἠθικὰ Νικομάχεια) Perseus (Greek + English)
          • Politics · Metaphysics · Organon Politics Metaphysics Perseus · Aristotle
      • Cynics, Cyrenaics & the Hellenistic Turn Κυνικοί · Κυρηναϊκοί The art of living — and the schools founded right at the 300 BCE boundary.
        • Diogenes of Sinope (Cynicism) ΔιογένηςDiogénēs c. 412–323 BCE
          With Antisthenes, founded Cynicism: virtue is the only good, lived in radical self-sufficiency and shameless freedom from convention (“I am a citizen of the world”).
          • via Diogenes Laërtius, Lives VI Wikisource · Lives, Bk VI
        • Epicurus (Epicureanism) ἘπίκουροςEpíkouros 341–270 BCE ~300
          Pleasure as freedom from pain and fear (ataraxia); an atomist, materialist ethics. Founded “the Garden” c. 306 BCE.
          • Letter to Menoeceus · Principal Doctrines Wikisource · Lives, Bk X
        • Zeno of Citium (Stoicism) Ζήνων ὁ ΚιτιεύςZēnōn c. 334–262 BCE ~300
          Founded Stoicism c. 300 BCE: live “according to nature” and reason; virtue alone is good, externals indifferent. Writings survive only in fragments.
          • via Diogenes Laërtius, Lives VII Wikisource · Lives, Bk VII

About this tree & its sources

Seven civilizations, more than 50 thinkers and texts. “Before 300 BCE” is read inclusively: a few figures (Aristotle, Xunzi, Han Feizi, Epicurus, Zeno of Citium) span or just cross the line and are marked ~300. The Indian and Mesopotamian/Egyptian traditions reach back over a millennium earlier.

Every “📜” link points to a freely readable online edition — in the original language where the script survives (Greek, Chinese, Hebrew, Sanskrit, Avestan), otherwise in a standard scholarly translation. Principal digital libraries used:
